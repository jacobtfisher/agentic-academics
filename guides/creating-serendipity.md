# Creating Serendipity

One of the most underrated uses of agentic AI in research is building systems that surface connections you wouldn't have found on your own — not replacing your thinking, but expanding the territory it covers.

## The Problem: Siloed Knowledge

Academic work naturally creates silos. You read deeply in your subfield. Your notes accumulate in folders organized by project. Your Zotero library is sorted by collection. Your manuscript drafts reference the papers you already know well. This is efficient, and to a degree it's necessary — deep expertise requires sustained attention to a bounded set of problems.

With that said, it's also a trap. The most generative research insights often come from unexpected connections: a method from one field applied to a question in another, a theoretical framework that reframes a stale debate, a dataset you didn't know existed that tests your hypothesis in a new context. Scholars have long recognized that disciplinary boundaries both enable and constrain — they produce depth at the cost of peripheral vision.

These connections depend on serendipity, on stumbling across the right idea at the right time. The problem is that serendipity doesn't scale. You can't stumble across things you never encounter.

## Agentic AI as a Serendipity Engine

Agentic AI tools can systematically create the conditions for serendipity — not by generating insights for you, but by widening the net of what you encounter and helping you notice patterns across your existing knowledge.

Here's the key insight: you already have more connections in your research than you realize. They're scattered across notes, papers, drafts, and half-formed ideas that you haven't had time to cross-reference. The bottleneck isn't a lack of material. It's the practical impossibility of holding your entire knowledge base in working memory and scanning for resonances across all of it simultaneously. AI tools are well suited to precisely this kind of broad-but-shallow search — the kind that surfaces candidates for your (deeper, slower, more evaluative) attention.

## Pattern: The Research Note Pipeline

One effective approach is a multi-stage pipeline that processes your research materials and surfaces connections over time.

### Stage 1: Seed Notes

Start with what you're already doing — reading papers, writing manuscript drafts, thinking about your research. As you work, flag ideas, questions, and connections that feel interesting but don't have an obvious home yet.

These seeds don't need to be polished. A sentence or two is fine: "The habit literature assumes utility maximization but doesn't account for wanting/liking dissociations — could this explain why people keep checking social media even when they report not enjoying it?"

### Stage 2: Expand and Connect

A scheduled skill (or a periodic manual session) takes your seed notes and does the following:

1. **Searches your reference library** for papers related to the seed idea — especially papers you tagged or annotated but haven't thought about recently
2. **Searches across your existing notes** for related concepts, looking for connections you haven't explicitly made
3. **Generates a structured research note** that maps the seed idea to relevant literature and existing threads in your work

The output isn't a finished product. It's a starting point for your own thinking — a note that says "here are five papers in your library that touch on this idea from different angles, and here's how they might connect." The value lies in the encounter, not in the AI's interpretation of it.

### Stage 3: Promote and Integrate

Periodically, you review the expanded notes. The ones that spark genuine insight get promoted into more durable forms: a section in a manuscript, a new research question, a connection added to an existing project note. The ones that don't sit in your notes, contributing to the ambient context that AI tools can draw on in future searches. Nothing is wasted — even the connections that don't immediately pay off enrich the substrate from which future connections emerge.

## Why This Works

The pipeline works because it automates the *encounter* while preserving the *judgment*. You're not asking AI to have insights on your behalf. You're asking it to put things in front of you that you might not have looked at together.

This mirrors how serendipity works in the physical world. A library browse works not because the books decide to be relevant, but because spatial proximity creates unexpected encounters. A conference hallway conversation works because it puts you in contact with someone outside your usual network. In both cases, the mechanism is exposure — being placed in the vicinity of ideas you weren't looking for. The pipeline creates that proximity digitally, across your entire library, note system, and manuscript archive.

## Practical Implementation

You don't need a complex system to start. Here's a minimal version:

1. **Keep a running list of seed ideas** — a single note file where you jot questions and half-formed connections as they occur to you
2. **Once a week, pick one seed** and ask Claude Code (with your Zotero MCP connected): "Here's an idea I've been thinking about. Search my library for papers that relate to this from different theoretical traditions. Also search my notes for existing threads that connect."
3. **Spend 20 minutes with the results.** Not writing — just reading and thinking. See if anything clicks.

As you get more comfortable, you can formalize this into a skill, schedule it to run automatically, or build a multi-stage pipeline. The infrastructure can grow with your needs.

## The Goal

The goal isn't to produce more notes. It's to make your existing knowledge work harder — to ensure that the paper you read three months ago gets surfaced when it's relevant to the idea you had this morning.

In a field that rewards specialization, tools that help you think across boundaries offer a genuine advantage. Not because they think for you, but because they help you encounter more of your own knowledge — moving beyond the constraints of project-organized silos in favor of a system that actively maps the connections among them.
