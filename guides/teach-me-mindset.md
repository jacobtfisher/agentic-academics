# Teach Me, Don't Do It For Me

The single most consequential decision you can make when working with an AI coding agent has nothing to do with prompting technique or tool configuration. It has to do with what you ask for.

Most people begin by asking AI to *do things*: "Write me a literature review." "Analyze this data." "Draft an introduction." And AI will do these things. Competently, quickly, and without complaint. That is the problem. The output arrives, but the thinking that should have produced it never happens.

## The Productive Struggle

Learning researchers Robert and Elizabeth Bjork coined the term "desirable difficulties" to describe a counterintuitive finding: learning strategies that feel slow and effortful in the moment produce better long-term retention and understanding than strategies that feel easy. Rereading your notes feels productive but generates false familiarity. Forcing yourself to recall information from memory feels frustrating but builds durable knowledge. The discomfort is the mechanism, not an obstacle to it.

Writing works in much the same way. As media theorist Walter Ong argued, writing is not merely recording thoughts you already have. It *restructures your thinking*. The act of translating a half-formed intuition into a sentence forces you to confront whether the idea is actually coherent, whether the logic holds under the pressure of articulation. The generation-inspection-revision loop that constitutes the writing process is where thinking happens.

When you ask AI to write for you, you skip that loop entirely. You get output, but you don't get understanding.

## What "Teach Me" Looks Like in Practice

The shift is simple in concept: instead of asking AI to produce deliverables, ask it to help you think.

**Instead of:** "Write a literature review on media multitasking and attention."

**Try:** "I'm trying to understand the current state of the media multitasking literature. I think the field is fragmented: there are attention-based accounts, habit-based accounts, and reward-based accounts that don't talk to each other much. Push back on that framing. What am I missing? What connections might I be overlooking?"

**Instead of:** "Analyze this dataset and tell me what's significant."

**Try:** "I have a 2x3 between-subjects design with these DVs. Walk me through how you'd approach the analysis. What assumptions should I check first? What would you look at before running any inferential tests?"

**Instead of:** "Draft a methods section for this study."

**Try:** "Here's my study design. Before I write the methods section, help me think about what a skeptical reviewer would flag. Are there design choices I should justify more carefully? Anything I'm taking for granted?"

In each case, you're still doing the cognitive work. AI becomes a sounding board, a devil's advocate, a well-read colleague who's always available. You generate the ideas; AI helps you inspect and refine them.

## When Delegation Is the Right Call

This isn't a blanket rule. There are plenty of tasks where handing off the work is both efficient and appropriate:

- **Formatting and boilerplate.** Converting citation formats, generating BibTeX entries, formatting tables. These are mechanical tasks where struggle teaches you nothing.
- **Data wrangling.** Reshaping datasets, merging files, cleaning variable names. If you understand what the transformation should do, having AI write the code is efficient, not lazy.
- **First-pass exploration.** Asking AI to summarize a paper you haven't read yet, or to sketch out what a dataset contains, can serve as a useful triage step before you invest your own time.
- **Scaffolding.** Having AI generate a project skeleton, a directory structure, or a template you'll fill in yourself.

The rule of thumb: **delegate the mechanical, engage with the conceptual.** If a task requires judgment, interpretation, or argumentation (the capacities that make you a researcher), do it yourself, with AI as your interlocutor.

## The Mentorship Angle

Computer science professor Ariel Rosenfeld recently wrote in *Science* about an uncomfortable realization: for a new research project, he was tempted to "hire" AI rather than recruit a graduate student. AI requires no ramp-up time, no weekly meetings, no emotional support. It delivers immediate returns.

With that said, Rosenfeld's own career started with a year of producing "nonsense" before he could contribute meaningfully. His adviser didn't give up on him. The slow, often frustrating transformation from novice to colleague is precisely what the academic system is designed to produce.

The "teach me" mindset extends beyond your own learning. It is about modeling the kind of engagement with AI that preserves space for that transformation. If faculty adopt a purely extractive relationship with AI (give me outputs, save me time), the implicit message to students is that the struggle is not worth it. If instead you use AI as a thinking partner, visibly, in your lab meetings and your teaching, you demonstrate that the cognitive work is the point. We think that distinction matters.

## Getting Started

If you're new to this, try one thing this week: take a question you'd normally ask AI to answer and rephrase it as a request for a conversation. Ask AI to challenge your assumptions, to explain trade-offs, to help you think through a decision rather than make it for you.

You'll be slower at first. That's the desirable difficulty doing its job.
